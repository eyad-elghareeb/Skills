# Clinical Vignette Patterns — High-Yield Board Presentations

This file contains curated clinical patterns organized by organ system. Each pattern represents a high-yield board presentation with the key findings that should be included in vignettes, the common differentials that make good distractors, and the typical question types that work best.

Use these patterns when generating questions to ensure clinical accuracy, appropriate difficulty, and board-relevance.

## Cardiology

### Acute Coronary Syndrome (STEMI)
**Classic presentation**: Middle-aged or older male (or postmenopausal female) with crushing substernal chest pain radiating to left arm/jaw, diaphoresis, nausea. ECG shows ST elevation in leads corresponding to the occluded artery.
**Key differentials for distractors**: Aortic dissection (tearing pain, BP differential between arms), pericarditis (positional pain, diffuse ST elevation with PR depression), GERD (burning, postprandial), costochondritis (reproducible chest wall tenderness), pulmonary embolism (pleuritic, tachycardia).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, next step in management (PCI vs. fibrinolysis), mechanism of troponin elevation, complication identification (ventricular free wall rupture, papillary muscle rupture, LV aneurysm — each presenting at different time points).

### Congestive Heart Failure
**Classic presentation**: Exertional dyspnea progressing to orthopnea and PND, peripheral edema, fatigue. S3 (systolic HF) or S4 (diastolic HF). Elevated BNP.
**Key differentials**: COPD exacerbation (wheezing, smoking history), pulmonary embolism (acute onset, pleuritic), pneumonia (fever, productive cough), cirrhosis (ascites out of proportion to edema, no pulmonary congestion).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis (HFrEF vs. HFpEF), mechanism of edema formation, drug of choice (ACE inhibitor/ARB/ARNI + beta-blocker + MRA + SGLT2i for HFrEF), acute management (diuretics, vasodilators).

### Atrial Fibrillation
**Classic presentation**: Irregularly irregular pulse, palpitations, possibly dyspnea. Associated with mitral valve disease, hyperthyroidism, alcohol ("holiday heart"), post-cardiac surgery.
**Key differentials**: Multifocal atrial tachycardia (multiple P wave morphologies), atrial flutter (sawtooth waves, regular rate), sinus arrhythmia (phasic with respiration, normal P waves).
**Best question types**: ECG interpretation, CHA2DS2-VASc scoring and anticoagulation decision, rate vs. rhythm control strategy, mechanism of stroke risk (stasis in left atrial appendage).

### Aortic Stenosis
**Classic presentation**: Exertional angina, syncope, and dyspnea (the classic triad — all three indicate advanced disease). Systolic ejection crescendo-decrescendo murmur radiating to the right upper sternal border, delayed carotid upstroke (pulsus parvus et tardus).
**Key differentials**: Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (murmur increases with Valsalva, decreases with handgrip — opposite of AS), aortic sclerosis (murmur without hemodynamic significance), pulmonary stenosis (murmur at left upper sternal border).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, severity assessment (valve area, mean gradient), timing of surgical intervention (symptomatic severe AS = Class I indication), murmur characteristics and maneuvers.

## Pulmonology

### Pulmonary Embolism
**Classic presentation**: Acute-onset dyspnea and pleuritic chest pain, tachycardia, hypoxia. Risk factors: recent surgery, immobilization, malignancy, OCP use, factor V Leiden. Wells score components.
**Key differentials**: Pneumonia (fever, productive cough, consolidation on CXR), pneumothorax (absent breath sounds unilaterally, tracheal deviation if tension), asthma exacerbation (wheezing, history of atopy), costochondritis (reproducible chest wall pain, normal imaging).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, appropriate diagnostic algorithm (D-dimer → CT-PA for moderate probability; directly to CT-PA for high probability), mechanism of hypoxia (V/Q mismatch), anticoagulation choice and duration.

### COPD Exacerbation
**Classic presentation**: Chronic smoker with progressive dyspnea, chronic cough, wheezing, prolonged expiratory phase, barrel chest. Exacerbation triggered by infection or air pollution.
**Key differentials**: Asthma (reversible, atopic history, younger onset), heart failure (BNP elevated, crackles, S3), bronchiectasis (daily copious sputum, recurrent infections, cystic fibrosis history).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis (emphysema vs. chronic bronchitis phenotype), mechanism of airflow limitation (loss of elastic recoil in emphysema), management (bronchodilators, steroids, antibiotics for Anthonisen criteria), oxygen therapy concerns (hypoxic drive — though this is debated, it's still board-relevant).

### Pneumonia (Community-acquired)
**Classic presentation**: Fever, productive cough, dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain. Lobar consolidation on CXR. "Typical" (S. pneumoniae) vs. "atypical" (Mycoplasma, Legionella, Chlamydia) patterns.
**Key differentials**: Pulmonary embolism (no fever typically, V/Q mismatch), TB (chronic, night sweats, weight loss, upper lobe predominance), lung abscess (cavity with air-fluid level, aspiration risk), PE with infarction.
**Best question types**: Pathogen identification based on clinical context, empiric antibiotic selection (outpatient vs. inpatient vs. ICU), Legionella clues (hyponatremia, GI symptoms, contaminated water), complication identification (empyema, abscess, ARDS).

### Obstructive Sleep Apnea
**Classic presentation**: Obese middle-aged male with daytime somnolence, loud snoring, witnessed apneas by bed partner, morning headaches. Neck circumference > 17 inches.
**Key differentials**: Narcolepsy (cataplexy, sleep attacks, hypocretin deficiency), idiopathic hypersomnia (no cataplexy, long unrefreshing sleep), insomnia (difficulty falling/staying asleep, not excessive sleepiness).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, complications (pulmonary hypertension, systemic hypertension, metabolic syndrome), treatment (CPAP first-line), mechanism of cardiovascular complications (sympathetic activation from recurrent hypoxemia).

## Gastroenterology

### Acute Pancreatitis
**Classic presentation**: Severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, vomiting, relief by leaning forward. Elevated lipase (> 3x upper limit of normal). Gallstones and alcohol are the top two causes.
**Key differentials**: Perforated peptic ulcer (rigid abdomen, free air on CXR), mesenteric ischemia (pain out of proportion to exam, metabolic acidosis), aortic dissection (tearing pain, BP differential), biliary colic (RUQ pain, self-limited, normal lipase).
**Best question types**: Etiology identification, Ranson/BISAP criteria for severity assessment, mechanism of pancreatic autodigestion (premature activation of trypsinogen), complications (pseudocyst at 4-6 weeks, infected necrosis, ARDS).

### Inflammatory Bowel Disease — Crohn Disease vs. Ulcerative Colitis
**Crohn classic presentation**: Crampy abdominal pain, chronic diarrhea (may be bloody but less consistently), weight loss, perianal disease (fistulas, fissures). Transmural inflammation, skip lesions, non-caseating granulomas. Can affect any part of GI tract (terminal ileum most common).
**UC classic presentation**: Bloody diarrhea, tenesmus, urgency. Superficial mucosal inflammation, continuous from rectum proximally, crypt abscesses. Limited to colon.
**Key differentials**: Infectious colitis (acute onset, travel history, specific pathogens), ischemic colitis (elderly, pain out of proportion, "thumbprinting" on imaging), diverticulitis (LLQ pain, older patient, CT shows diverticula with fat stranding).
**Best question types**: Crohn vs. UC distinction, extraintestinal manifestations (both share many: erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum, axial arthritis; some are disease-specific: primary sclerosing cholangitis more with UC, fistulas only in Crohn), pharmacotherapy (step-up vs. top-down therapy, anti-TNF agents), cancer risk (UC → colon cancer surveillance).

### Cirrhosis and Portal Hypertension
**Classic presentation**: Chronic liver disease stigmata (palmar erythema, spider angiomas, gynecomastia, caput medusae), ascites, hepatic encephalopathy (asterixis, altered mental status), jaundice, coagulopathy.
**Key differentials**: Right heart failure (elevated JVP, hepatojugular reflux, no stigmata of chronic liver disease), Budd-Chiari syndrome (hepatomegaly, ascites, hepatic vein thrombosis), nephrotic syndrome (generalized edema, proteinuria > 3.5 g/day).
**Best question types**: Complication management (ascites: spironolactone first, then furosemide in 100:40 ratio; hepatic encephalopathy: lactulose + rifaximin; variceal bleeding: octreotide + endoscopic band ligation), mechanism of hyperaldosteronism (decreased hepatic metabolism of aldosterone), Child-Pugh classification.

## Nephrology / Urology

### Acute Kidney Injury
**Classic presentation**: Rising creatinine with or without oliguria. Must distinguish pre-renal (BUN/Cr > 20:1, FENa < 1%), intrinsic (ATN: muddy brown casts, FENa > 2%; AIN: WBC casts, eosinophils; glomerulonephritis: RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs), and post-renal (obstruction, hydronephrosis on ultrasound).
**Key differentials for distractors**: Distinguish among the three categories of AKI and among the subtypes of intrinsic AKI.
**Best question types**: Classification of AKI type, mechanism of pre-renal azotemia, nephrotoxic drug identification (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast, ACE inhibitors in bilateral RAS), urine sediment interpretation.

### Renal Tubular Acidosis
**Type 1 (Distal)**: Inability to secrete H+ in collecting duct. Metabolic acidosis with alkaline urine (pH > 5.5), hypokalemia, nephrocalcinosis, kidney stones. Associated with Sjögren syndrome, SLE, amphotericin B.
**Type 2 (Proximal)**: Impaired HCO3- reabsorption in proximal tubule. Metabolic acidosis with urine pH that can be < 5.5 (once serum HCO3- is sufficiently low). Associated with Fanconi syndrome (multiple proximal tubule defects), carbonic anhydrase inhibitors.
**Type 4**: Aldosterone deficiency or resistance. Hyperkalemia with metabolic acidosis. Associated with diabetes (hyporeninemic hypoaldosteronism), ACE inhibitors, spironolactone.
**Best question types**: RTA classification, mechanism of each type, urine pH and potassium patterns, associated conditions and drugs.

## Neurology

### Stroke — Ischemic
**Classic presentation**: Sudden onset focal neurologic deficit corresponding to a vascular territory. MCA: contralateral face/arm > leg weakness, aphasia (dominant hemisphere), neglect (non-dominant). ACA: contralateral leg > arm weakness. PCA: contralateral homonymous hemianopia with macular sparing. Vertebrobasilar: vertigo, ataxia, diplopia, crossed findings.
**Key differentials**: Hemorrhagic stroke (headache, vomiting, very high BP, no CT hypodensity initially), TIA (symptoms resolve within 24 hours, typically < 1 hour), hypoglycemia (can mimic any focal deficit), Todd paralysis (post-seizure deficit that resolves), brain tumor (progressive, not sudden).
**Best question types**: Vascular territory identification, tPA eligibility criteria (within 4.5 hours, no hemorrhage on CT, no contraindications), secondary prevention (antiplatelet for non-cardioembolic, anticoagulation for cardioembolic), mechanism of penumbra.

### Multiple Sclerosis
**Classic presentation**: Young woman (20-40, F:M = 2:1) with relapsing-remitting neurologic symptoms separated in time AND space. Optic neuritis (pain with eye movement, visual loss), internuclear ophthalmoplegia (impaired adduction, nystagmus in abducting eye), Lhermitte sign, Uhthoff phenomenon (worsening with heat).
**Key differentials**: Neuromyelitis optica (more severe optic neuritis and longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis, anti-AQP4 antibody), ADEM (monophasic, post-infectious), CNS lymphoma (solid lesions, older patients), vitamin B12 deficiency (subacute combined degeneration, no relapsing-remitting pattern).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis (McDonald criteria), CSF findings (oligoclonal bands, elevated IgG index), MRI findings (periventricular, juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord lesions), disease-modifying therapy selection, mechanism of demyelination.

### Guillain-Barré Syndrome
**Classic presentation**: Progressive symmetric ascending weakness with areflexia, 2-4 weeks after a respiratory or GI infection (Campylobacter jejuni is the classic trigger). May progress to respiratory failure requiring intubation.
**Key differentials**: Myasthenia gravis (fatigable weakness, ptosis, normal reflexes, anti-AChR antibody), botulism (descending paralysis, cranial nerve involvement, no fever), transverse myelitis (sensory level, bowel/bladder dysfunction), tick paralysis (ascending weakness, tick attached).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, CSF findings (albuminocytologic dissociation — elevated protein without pleocytosis), treatment (IVIG or plasmapheresis — do NOT use steroids), respiratory monitoring (forced vital capacity, NIF), mechanism of molecular mimicry (anti-ganglioside antibodies after Campylobacter).

## Psychiatry

### Major Depressive Disorder
**Classic presentation**: ≥ 2 weeks of depressed mood or anhedonia plus SIGECAPS (Sleep changes, Interest loss, Guilt, Energy loss, Concentration loss, Appetite changes, Psychomotor changes, Suicidal ideation). Must cause functional impairment.
**Key differentials**: Bipolar depression (history of mania/hypomania — MUST screen before prescribing antidepressants), dysthymia (chronic, milder, ≥ 2 years), adjustment disorder (stressor-related, < 6 months after stressor), grief (waves of sadness, preserved self-esteem, not constant).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, screening for bipolar disorder before treatment, first-line pharmacotherapy (SSRIs — fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram), mechanism of antidepressant action (increasing monoamine availability), SSRI side effects and serotonin syndrome.

### Schizophrenia
**Classic presentation**: ≥ 6 months of symptoms including delusions, hallucinations (auditory most common), disorganized speech, catatonic behavior, and negative symptoms (flat affect, alogia, avolition, anhedonia, social withdrawal). Functional decline.
**Key differentials**: Schizoaffective disorder (mood episodes concurrent with psychotic symptoms), bipolar disorder with psychotic features (psychosis only during mood episodes), brief psychotic disorder (< 1 month), substance-induced psychosis (history of use, toxicology screen), delusional disorder (non-bizarre delusions without other psychotic features).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, positive vs. negative symptoms, first-line treatment (second-generation antipsychotics), mechanism of antipsychotic action (D2 blockade), side effects (metabolic syndrome with atypicals, EPS with typicals, tardive dyskinesia, neuroleptic malignant syndrome).

## Endocrinology

### Diabetic Ketoacidosis
**Classic presentation**: Type 1 diabetic with nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid), fruity breath odor, dehydration, altered mental status. Glucose > 250, pH < 7.3, bicarbonate < 18, positive serum ketones, anion gap metabolic acidosis.
**Key differentials**: Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (type 2 DM, glucose > 600, no significant ketosis, profound dehydration, pH > 7.3), alcoholic ketoacidosis (history of heavy alcohol use, normal or low glucose, ketonemia), starvation ketosis (mild ketosis, normal glucose, no acidosis), lactic acidosis (elevated lactate, different context).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis and lab interpretation, fluid resuscitation protocol (normal saline first, then switch to D5 when glucose < 200-250), insulin therapy, potassium management (total body K+ is depleted despite normal/High serum K+ initially — must supplement once K+ < 5.5), mechanism of anion gap (ketone bodies as unmeasured anions).

### Thyroid Disorders
**Hyperthyroidism/Graves**: Weight loss, heat intolerance, palpitations, tremor, anxiety, diarrhea, diffuse goiter, exophthalmos (Graves-specific), pretibial myxedema (Graves-specific). TSH low, free T4 high, positive TSI.
**Hypothyroidism**: Weight gain, cold intolerance, fatigue, constipation, dry skin, bradycardia, delayed reflexes, periorbital puffiness, non-pitting edema (myxedema). TSH high, free T4 low.
**Key differentials**: Subacute thyroiditis (painful thyroid, transient thyrotoxicosis followed by hypothyroidism), factitious hyperthyroidism (surreptitious levothyroxine ingestion, low thyroglobulin), TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma (rare, TSH inappropriately normal or elevated with high T4).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, lab interpretation, treatment (methimazole for Graves, levothyroxine for hypothyroidism), thyroid storm management, mechanism of Graves ophthalmopathy (TSH receptor antibodies stimulating retroorbital fibroblasts).

### Adrenal Insufficiency
**Primary (Addison)**: Hypotension, hyperpigmentation (due to elevated ACTH/MSH), hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, fatigue. Low cortisol, high ACTH. Caused by autoimmune destruction (most common in developed countries), TB (most common worldwide), metastatic cancer.
**Secondary**: No hyperpigmentation (low ACTH), hyponatremia but NORMAL potassium (aldosterone intact — RAAS is independent of ACTH), hypoglycemia. Low cortisol, low ACTH. Caused by chronic exogenous glucocorticoid use (most common), pituitary disease.
**Key differentials**: SIADH (euvolemic hyponatremia, no hyperkalemia, normal cortisol), psychogenic polydipsia (low serum osmolality, dilute urine), renal salt wasting (high urine sodium, normal cortisol and ACTH).
**Best question types**: Primary vs. secondary distinction, ACTH stimulation test interpretation, acute adrenal crisis management (IV hydrocortisone + fluid resuscitation), mechanism of hyperpigmentation (ACTH shares a precursor with MSH), mineralocorticoid considerations (fludrocortisone needed in primary but not secondary).

## Hematology / Oncology

### Deep Vein Thrombosis / Hypercoagulable States
**Classic presentation**: Unilateral leg swelling, pain, warmth, erythema. Homan sign is unreliable. Confirm with compression ultrasonography.
**Key differentials**: Cellulitis (fever, erythema with clear borders, leukocytosis), ruptured Baker cyst (sudden onset, calf pain, history of knee arthritis), superficial thrombophlebitis (tender cord-like vein, no deep system involvement), muscle tear (history of injury, no swelling).
**Inherited hypercoagulable states for questions**: Factor V Leiden (most common — resistance to activated protein C), prothrombin G20210A mutation, protein C deficiency, protein S deficiency, antithrombin III deficiency (heparin resistance).
**Acquired**: Antiphospholipid syndrome, malignancy, OCP use, pregnancy, immobility, nephrotic syndrome.
**Best question types**: Etiology identification, anticoagulation choice (DOACs vs. warfarin vs. heparin), duration of therapy (provoked vs. unprovoked), hypercoagulable workup (when to order, timing relative to anticoagulation — do not test while on anticoagulants as it affects results).

### Leukemia — Acute
**AML classic presentation**: Middle-aged or older adult with fatigue (anemia), infections (neutropenia), bleeding (thrombocytopenia). Auer rods on peripheral smear. DIC association (especially M3/acute promyelocytic leukemia). t(15;17) in APL — treat with all-trans retinoic acid (ATRA) + arsenic trioxide.
**ALL classic presentation**: Children (peak 2-5 years) with bone pain, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, cytopenias. Most common childhood cancer. Associated with Down syndrome.
**Key differentials**: Chronic leukemias (insidious onset, high WBC with mature cells), infectious mononucleosis (atypical lymphocytes, positive monospot), aplastic anemia (pancytopenia without circulating blasts).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, immunophenotyping, cytogenetic associations, treatment, mechanism of APL and ATRA (differentiation therapy — ATRA degrades the PML-RARα fusion protein), tumor lysis syndrome.

## Musculoskeletal / Rheumatology

### Rheumatoid Arthritis
**Classic presentation**: Symmetric polyarthritis of small joints (MCP, PIP, wrists) with morning stiffness > 1 hour. Ulnar deviation, swan-neck deformities, boutonnière deformities. RF positive (70-80%), anti-CCP positive (more specific). Extra-articular: rheumatoid nodules, interstitial lung disease, vasculitis, Felty syndrome (RA + splenomegaly + neutropenia).
**Key differentials**: Osteoarthritis (asymmetric, DIP involvement, Heberden nodes, no prolonged morning stiffness, normal inflammatory markers), psoriatic arthritis (psoriasis, DIP involvement, nail pitting, pencil-in-cup deformity), SLE (arthralgias without erosions, malar rash, serositis), gout (monoarticular initially, podagra, crystals).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, serologic marker interpretation, pharmacotherapy (Methotrexate is first-line DMARD, add biologic if inadequate response), mechanism of joint destruction (TNF-alpha, IL-6, osteoclast activation via RANKL), extra-articular manifestations.

### Gout
**Classic presentation**: Acute monoarticular arthritis, classically first MTP (podagra). Exquisite tenderness, erythema, swelling. Triggered by alcohol, purine-rich foods, diuretics. Negatively birefringent needle-shaped crystals (monosodium urate) under polarized light.
**Key differentials**: Pseudogout (calcium pyrophosphate crystals, positively birefringent, knee most common, rhomboid-shaped), septic arthritis (fever, very high WBC in synovial fluid > 50,000, Gram stain/culture positive), cellulitis (overlying skin infection, no joint effusion), OA (chronic, non-inflammatory synovial fluid).
**Best question types**: Crystal identification, acute treatment (NSAIDs, colchicine, steroids — do NOT start or change urate-lowering therapy during acute flare), chronic management (allopurol for overproducers, probenecid for underexcretors), mechanism of allopurinol (xanthine oxidase inhibitor), febuxostat (non-purine xanthine oxidase inhibitor).

## Reproductive / OB-GYN

### Ectopic Pregnancy
**Classic presentation**: Reproductive-age woman with amenorrhea, vaginal bleeding, and abdominal pain. Positive pregnancy test (β-hCG). Risk factors: prior ectopic, PID/tubal scarring, IUD use, tubal surgery, smoking.
**Key differentials**: Threatened abortion (uterine pregnancy with bleeding, closed os), miscarriage (passage of tissue, open os), ruptured ovarian cyst (sudden pain, no amenorrhea, negative pregnancy test), ovarian torsion (sudden pain, nausea, enlarged ovary on ultrasound).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis (transvaginal ultrasound — empty uterus with adnexal mass), management (methotrexate if stable and qualifying criteria vs. surgery if ruptured/hemodynamically unstable), mechanism of methotrexate (inhibits dihydrofolate reductase, prevents DNA synthesis in rapidly dividing trophoblastic tissue), β-hCG monitoring after treatment.

### Preeclampsia
**Classic presentation**: New-onset hypertension (> 140/90 after 20 weeks gestation) with proteinuria OR end-organ dysfunction (thrombocytopenia, renal insufficiency, elevated liver enzymes, pulmonary edema, cerebral/visual symptoms). May progress to eclampsia (seizures).
**Key differentials**: Gestational hypertension (HTN without proteinuria or end-organ damage), chronic hypertension (pre-existing, diagnosed before pregnancy or before 20 weeks), HELLP syndrome (variant of severe preeclampsia — Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, Low Platelets).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis and severity classification, definitive treatment (delivery — the only cure), magnesium sulfate for seizure prophylaxis, mechanism of preeclampsia pathophysiology (abnormal placentation → placental ischemia → anti-angiogenic factors → endothelial dysfunction), antihypertensive choices in pregnancy (labetalol, hydralazine — avoid ACE inhibitors and ARBs).

## Infectious Disease

### Tuberculosis
**Classic presentation**: Chronic cough (> 3 weeks), hemoptysis, night sweats, weight loss, fever. Risk factors: immunosuppression, HIV, homelessness, incarceration, immigration from endemic area, alcoholism. Upper lobe preference (higher oxygen tension).
**Key differentials**: Lung cancer (mass on imaging, older smoker, no fever pattern), fungal pneumonia (histoplasmosis in Ohio/Mississippi River Valley, coccidioidomycosis in Southwest), sarcoidosis (non-caseating granulomas, bilateral hilar adenopathy, elevated ACE level), atypical mycobacteria (MAC in HIV, not contagious).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis (sputum AFB smear and culture, nucleic acid amplification test, interferon-gamma release assay for latent TB), treatment (RIPE: Rifampin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol for 2 months, then RI for 4 months), mechanism of drug resistance, side effects (INH: peripheral neuropathy — prevent with B6; RIF: orange discoloration of body fluids, CYP450 inducer; PZA: hepatotoxicity, hyperuricemia; EMB: optic neurithis — check visual acuity).

### HIV / AIDS
**Classic presentation**: Opportunistic infections and malignancies based on CD4 count. > 500: relatively intact immunity. 200-500: oral candidiasis, Kaposi sarcoma (HHV-8), lymphoma. < 200: Pneumocystis pneumonia, toxoplasmosis, cryptococcal meningitis. < 50: CMV retinitis, MAC, CNS lymphoma.
**Key differentials**: For PCP: community-acquired pneumonia (bacterial, productive cough, lobar consolidation), TB (AFB positive, upper lobes), pneumothorax (absent breath sounds). For CNS lesions: toxoplasmosis (ring-enhancing lesions, anti-Toxo IgG positive) vs. CNS lymphoma (single lesion, EBV positive in CSF).
**Best question types**: Opportunistic infection based on CD4 count, PCP prophylaxis (TMP-SMX when CD4 < 200), ART initiation (all patients regardless of CD4 count), mechanism of entry inhibitors, drug interactions and side effects.

## Immunology

### Anaphylaxis
**Classic presentation**: Rapid onset (minutes to hours) of urticaria, angioedema, bronchospasm, hypotension after exposure to allergen (food, drug, insect sting, latex). Histamine and tryptase release from mast cells.
**Key differentials**: Vasovagal syncope (no urticaria, bronchospasm, or angioedema; bradycardia), hereditary angioedema (C1 esterase inhibitor deficiency, no urticaria, recurrent, onset over hours), carcinoid syndrome (flushing, diarrhea, wheezing — but not urticaria, elevated 5-HIAA), panic attack (no hypotension or urticaria).
**Best question types**: Diagnosis, first-line treatment (IM epinephrine — not steroids, not antihistamines as first-line), mechanism (IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation), biphasic reaction awareness, secondary measures (IV fluids, antihistamines, steroids as adjuncts).

### Autoimmune Disease Associations
**Type IV hypersensitivity**: Contact dermatitis (nickel, poison ivy), PPD skin test, graft rejection. T-cell mediated, delayed (48-72 hours). No antibody involvement.
**Type II hypersensitivity**: Goodpasture syndrome (anti-GBM), myasthenia gravis (anti-AChR), Graves disease (anti-TSH receptor — stimulating antibody), pemphigus vulgaris (anti-desmoglein). Antibody-mediated cytotoxicity.
**Type III hypersensitivity**: SLE, post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis, serum sickness, Arthus reaction. Immune complex deposition.
**Best question types**: Hypersensitivity type classification, mechanism of tissue damage, specific autoantibody identification, clinical manifestations of each type.

## Pharmacology Integration

Cross-cutting pharmacology questions should integrate drug mechanisms with clinical scenarios. High-yield drug classes for board questions:

### Antimicrobials
- **Penicillins**: Mechanism (bind PBPs → inhibit transpeptidation → weaken cell wall). Key adverse: hypersensitivity, interstitial nephritis.
- **Vancomycin**: Mechanism (binds D-ala-D-ala → prevents transglycosylation). Red man syndrome (histamine release, not IgE — treat by slowing infusion). MRSA coverage.
- **Fluoroquinolones**: Mechanism (inhibit DNA gyrase/topoisomerase IV). Tendon rupture, QT prolongation, cartilage damage in children.
- **Macrolides**: Mechanism (bind 50S → block translocation). CYP3A4 inhibitors (erythromycin > clarithromycin). QT prolongation.
- **Aminoglycosides**: Mechanism (bind 30S → misreading of mRNA). Nephrotoxicity (proximal tubule), ototoxicity (vestibular and auditory). Concentration-dependent killing, post-antibiotic effect.

### Cardiovascular Drugs
- **ACE inhibitors**: Mechanism (prevent ANG I → ANG II conversion). Indications (HFrEF, diabetic nephropathy, HTN). Side effects: cough (bradykinin accumulation), angioedema, hyperkalemia, acute renal failure in bilateral RAS.
- **Beta-blockers**: Mechanism (competitive antagonism of β-adrenergic receptors). Contraindications: decompensated HF, severe asthma, heart block. Carvedilol/metoprolol succinate for HFrEF (not atenolol). Overdose treatment: glucagon.
- **Statins**: Mechanism (inhibit HMG-CoA reductase). Most effective LDL-lowering agents. Side effects: myopathy, hepatotoxicity. Contraindicated in pregnancy.

## Biochemistry Integration

High-yield biochemistry patterns that appear in clinical vignettes:

### Inborn Errors of Metabolism
- **Phenylketonuria**: Deficient phenylalanine hydroxylase → elevated phenylalanine → intellectual disability if untreated. Musty body odor, fair skin (decreased melanin due to tyrosine deficiency). Treatment: phenylalanine-restricted diet. Screened at birth.
- **Galactosemia**: Deficient galactose-1-phosphate uridyltransferase → accumulation of galactose-1-phosphate. Cataracts, hepatomegaly, jaundice, E. coli sepsis in neonates. Treatment: galactose-free diet.
- **Maple syrup urine disease**: Deficient branched-chain α-keto acid dehydrogenase → elevated leucine, isoleucine, valine. Maple syrup odor of urine. Neurotoxicity, cerebral edema. Treatment: restricted branched-chain amino acids.

### Vitamin Deficiencies
- **Vitamin A**: Night blindness, xerophthalmia, Bitot spots, follicular hyperkeratosis. Excess: hepatotoxicity, teratogenicity, pseudotumor cerebri.
- **Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)**: Beriberi (wet: high-output HF; dry: peripheral neuropathy), Wernicke encephalopathy (ataxia, nystagmus, confusion), Korsakoff psychosis (anterograde amnesia, confabulation). Seen in alcoholism, malnutrition, bariatric surgery.
- **Vitamin B12**: Macrocytic anemia, hypersegmented neutrophils, subacute combined degeneration (dorsal columns + lateral corticospinal tracts), positive Romberg. Causes: pernicious anemia (anti-intrinsic factor), terminal ileum disease, Diphyllobothrium latum, vegans. Schilling test historically; now anti-intrinsic factor and anti-parietal cell antibodies.
- **Vitamin C**: Scurvy — perifollicular hemorrhage, bleeding gums, poor wound healing, corkscrew hairs. Required for proline hydroxylation in collagen synthesis.
- **Vitamin D**: Rickets (children: craniotabes, rachitic rosary, bowed legs), osteomalacia (adults: diffuse bone pain, fractures). Deficiency from malabsorption, lack of sunlight, chronic kidney disease (decreased 1α-hydroxylation).

## Using This Reference

When generating questions on a specific topic:

1. Find the relevant organ system section above
2. Identify the "Classic presentation" — use this as the backbone of your vignette
3. Use the "Key differentials" to construct your distractors — these are the conditions most likely to be confused with the correct answer
4. Use the "Best question types" to select your lead-in format and ensure you're testing the most board-relevant cognitive skill
5. Cross-reference with the question templates in `question-templates.md` to apply the correct structural format
6. Always add clinical nuance beyond what's listed here — use these patterns as a starting framework, then enrich with specific lab values, medication details, and pertinent negatives to create a unique, challenging question
