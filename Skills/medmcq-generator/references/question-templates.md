# Question Templates — Best of Five Medical MCQs

This file contains detailed templates for each question type, with worked examples showing proper vignette construction, distractor design, and explanation writing.

---

## Template 1: Most Likely Diagnosis

**Lead-in format**: "Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?"

**Vignette construction strategy**:
- Present a patient with a constellation of symptoms that form a recognizable pattern
- Include 2-3 findings that strongly suggest the diagnosis
- Include 1-2 findings that help differentiate from the closest mimics
- Place at least one pertinent negative to rule out the most common differential

**Distractor strategy**:
- Distractor 1: The most common differential diagnosis (the condition most easily confused with the correct answer)
- Distractor 2: A condition that shares one key feature but differs in another critical aspect
- Distractor 3: A condition from the same organ system or presenting similarly
- Distractor 4: A less common but still plausible condition that shares some features

### Example

**Question 1**

A 55-year-old man presents to the emergency department with a 2-day history of worsening shortness of breath and orthopnea. He reports that he can no longer lie flat without feeling suffocated and has been sleeping upright in a chair for the past 2 nights. He also notes a 1-week history of bilateral ankle swelling that has progressively worsened. His medical history is significant for an anterior wall myocardial infarction 8 months ago, hypertension, and type 2 diabetes mellitus. He takes metoprolol, lisinopril, aspirin, and metformin. On physical examination, blood pressure is 148/92 mm Hg, heart rate is 102/min, respiratory rate is 24/min, and SpO2 is 91% on room air. Jugular venous distension is present at 12 cm H2O. Cardiac auscultation reveals an S3 gallop and no murmurs. Bibasilar crackles are heard over the lung fields. There is 3+ pitting edema bilaterally to the mid-shins. BNP is 1,450 pg/mL (ref: < 100). ECG shows Q waves in V1-V4 with no acute ST changes.

**Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?**

A) Acute pulmonary embolism
B) Constrictive pericarditis
C) Decompensated heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
D) Exacerbation of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease
E) Pneumocystis pneumonia

**Correct Answer: C. Decompensated heart failure with reduced ejection fraction**

**Explanation:**

This patient presents with decompensated heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), indicated by the classic triad of orthopnea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, bilateral lower extremity edema, and volume overload signs on examination.

The patient's history of anterior wall MI 8 months ago is the critical predisposing event — anterior infarctions cause significant left ventricular systolic dysfunction, which is the most common cause of HFrEF. The orthopnea (requiring upright sleep) and PND are hallmark symptoms of left-sided heart failure. The elevated JVP (12 cm H2O), S3 gallop (indicative of rapid ventricular filling in a volume-overloaded, dilated ventricle), bibasilar crackles (pulmonary edema), and 3+ pitting edema (right-sided heart failure signs) together indicate biventricular failure. The markedly elevated BNP (1,450 pg/mL) is strongly consistent with heart failure as the cause of dyspnea (BNP > 400 in the appropriate clinical context is highly suggestive of HF). The Q waves in V1-V4 confirm prior anterior infarction, which explains the underlying systolic dysfunction.

**Why each option is incorrect:**
- A) Acute pulmonary embolism — While PE can cause acute dyspnea and tachycardia, this patient has multiple findings pointing toward heart failure rather than PE: the S3 gallop, bibasilar crackles, markedly elevated BNP, and progressive symptoms over days (PE typically presents more abruptly). PE would also more likely present with hypoxia out of proportion to exam findings, pleuritic chest pain, or signs of deep vein thrombosis. The misconception tested is confusing acute dyspnea etiologies without using the full clinical picture.
- B) Constrictive pericarditis — Constrictive pericarditis can cause JVD and peripheral edema, but it does not cause pulmonary crackles, S3 gallop, or significantly elevated BNP. It typically presents with signs of right-sided heart failure (JVD, edema, ascites) WITHOUT pulmonary congestion, and the patient would have a pericardial knock rather than an S3. The misconception tested is failure to distinguish between causes of systemic venous congestion.
- D) Exacerbation of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease — Although COPD exacerbation can cause dyspnea and tachypnea, this patient has no history of COPD, no wheezing or prolonged expiratory phase, and the S3 gallop with BNP of 1,450 pg/mL make a primary pulmonary etiology extremely unlikely. The misconception tested is attributing dyspnea to a smoking-associated lung disease without considering cardiac causes.
- E) Pneumocystis pneumonia — PCP typically occurs in immunocompromised patients (HIV with CD4 < 200, transplant recipients, patients on immunosuppressive therapy). This patient has no documented immunosuppression, and the S3 gallop, JVD, and BNP elevation are inconsistent with an isolated pulmonary infection. The misconception tested is over-considering rare diagnoses without epidemiologic risk factors.

**Educational Objective:** Identify the clinical features that distinguish decompensated heart failure from other causes of acute dyspnea.

**Key Concept:** In a patient with prior myocardial infarction, the combination of orthopnea, S3 gallop, bibasilar crackles, elevated JVP, and BNP > 400 pg/mL is diagnostic of decompensated heart failure. The S3 gallop is specific for volume overload in the setting of systolic dysfunction and helps distinguish cardiac from pulmonary causes of dyspnea.

## Template 2: Most Appropriate Next Step in Management

**Lead-in format**: "Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?"

**Vignette construction strategy**:
- Present a patient with a clear diagnosis (or enough information to reach one)
- Create clinical urgency or a decision point
- Make the management step dependent on correct identification of the diagnosis AND knowledge of the treatment algorithm
- Ensure the question tests the ORDER of management, not just knowledge of treatments

**Distractor strategy**:
- Distractor 1: A treatment that is appropriate but comes LATER in the algorithm (jumping ahead)
- Distractor 2: A treatment for a different condition with a similar presentation
- Distractor 3: An appropriate diagnostic test when the question asks for a management step (or vice versa)
- Distractor 4: A treatment that was historically used but is no longer first-line, or a treatment that is appropriate for a different severity/stage

### Example

**Question 2**

A 34-year-old woman presents to the clinic with a 3-week history of fatigue, joint pain involving her wrists and knees bilaterally, and a rash across her cheeks and bridge of her nose that worsens with sun exposure. She also reports mild chest pain that is worse when lying flat and improves when sitting forward. She has no significant past medical history and takes no medications. On examination, temperature is 37.8°C (100°F), blood pressure is 128/78 mm Hg, and heart rate is 88/min. There is an erythematous, symmetric rash over the malar region sparing the nasolabial folds. Cardiac auscultation reveals a three-component friction rub. The wrists and knees are tender and swollen bilaterally with decreased range of motion. Laboratory studies show: WBC 3,200/mm³ (ref: 4,500-11,000), hemoglobin 10.2 g/dL (ref: 12-16), platelets 148,000/mm³ (ref: 150,000-400,000), ESR 48 mm/hr (ref: 0-20), urinalysis shows 2+ proteinuria with no hematuria or casts.

**Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in management?**

A) Begin cyclophosphamide therapy
B) Begin high-dose glucocorticoid therapy
C) Begin hydroxychloroquine and low-dose glucocorticoid therapy
D) Order renal biopsy
E) Start azathioprine monotherapy

**Correct Answer: C. Begin hydroxychloroquine and low-dose glucocorticoid therapy**

**Explanation:**

This patient presents with a classic picture of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) with moderate disease activity. The diagnosis is supported by her malar rash (sparing nasolabial folds — characteristic of SLE and distinguishing it from rosacea), symmetric polyarthritis, pericarditis (pleuritic chest pain improved by sitting forward + friction rub), cytopenias (leukopenia, anemia, borderline thrombocytopenia), and proteinuria suggesting renal involvement.

For SLE with moderate activity — defined as constitutional symptoms, arthritis, serositis, and mild cytopenias WITHOUT organ-threatening disease — the appropriate initial management is hydroxychloroquine (the backbone of SLE therapy for all patients) combined with low-dose glucocorticoids for symptom control and inflammation suppression. Hydroxychloroquine reduces flares, improves long-term outcomes, and has a favorable safety profile. Low-dose prednisone (typically ≤ 10 mg/day) is used for moderate symptoms while waiting for hydroxychloroquine's delayed onset of action (which takes 2-3 months).

**Why each option is incorrect:**
- A) Begin cyclophosphamide therapy — Cyclophosphamide is reserved for severe, organ-threatening SLE, particularly Class III/IV proliferative lupus nephritis, CNS lupus, or severe pulmonary hemorrhage. This patient has moderate (not severe) disease. The misconception tested is over-treating moderate SLE with immunosuppressive therapy reserved for life-threatening manifestations.
- B) Begin high-dose glucocorticoid therapy — High-dose glucocorticoids (≥ 1 mg/kg/day prednisone or IV methylprednisolone) are indicated for severe, organ-threatening SLE (e.g., lupus nephritis Class III/IV, CNS vasculitis, severe hemolytic anemia). This patient's moderate symptoms do not warrant high-dose steroids, which carry significant morbidity (osteonecrosis, infection, diabetes, etc.). The misconception tested is using excessive immunosuppression for moderate disease when low-dose therapy is sufficient.
- D) Order renal biopsy — While the proteinuria suggests renal involvement, a biopsy is not the immediate next step in this patient with moderate disease activity. Renal biopsy is indicated when there is significant proteinuria (> 0.5 g/day) and the result would change management (i.e., before initiating immunosuppressive therapy for lupus nephritis). However, before performing an invasive biopsy, the initial step is to start appropriate medical therapy for moderate SLE. If proteinuria persists or worsens despite therapy, then biopsy would be warranted. The misconception tested is prioritizing invasive diagnostics over initiating established first-line therapy.
- E) Start azathioprine monotherapy — Azathioprine is a steroid-sparing agent used as adjunctive therapy in SLE, particularly for patients who cannot taper steroids or who have moderate disease requiring long-term immunosuppression. It is not appropriate as initial monotherapy; hydroxychloroquine remains the cornerstone of SLE treatment for all patients. The misconception tested is using a second-line immunosuppressant as first-line treatment.

**Educational Objective:** Determine the appropriate initial management for moderate systemic lupus erythematosus.

**Key Concept:** Moderate SLE (arthritis, serositis, rash, cytopenias without organ-threatening disease) is initially managed with hydroxychloroquine plus low-dose glucocorticoids. High-dose glucocorticoids and cytotoxic agents (cyclophosphamide) are reserved for severe, organ-threatening manifestations such as proliferative lupus nephritis or CNS involvement.

## Template 3: Mechanism of Disease / Drug

**Lead-in format**: "Which of the following best describes the mechanism of [the patient's condition / the drug most likely prescribed]?"

**Vignette construction strategy**:
- Present a patient with a condition that has a well-defined pathophysiologic mechanism
- The vignette should provide enough clinical information to identify the diagnosis
- The question then tests whether the learner understands WHY the condition occurs, not just THAT it occurs
- Alternatively, present a patient who needs a specific drug and test the drug's mechanism

**Distractor strategy**:
- Distractor 1: An adjacent step in the same pathway (close but wrong step)
- Distractor 2: The mechanism of a related condition with similar presentation
- Distractor 3: The mechanism of a drug used to treat the condition (confusing cause and treatment)
- Distractor 4: A real mechanism that is unrelated to the condition described

### Example

**Question 3**

A 62-year-old man with a 40-pack-year smoking history presents to his primary care physician with a 3-month history of progressive fatigue, weight gain of 8 kg, and easy bruising. He notes that his face has become rounder and he has developed purple stretch marks on his abdomen. He also reports new-onset back pain in the mid-thoracic region. Blood pressure is 162/98 mm Hg. Physical examination reveals central obesity with thin extremities, moon facies, dorsal cervical fat pad, and purple abdominal striae. A 4 cm firm, non-tender mass is palpable in the right upper lobe of the lung. Laboratory studies show: fasting glucose 188 mg/dL (ref: 70-100), WBC 14,500/mm³ (ref: 4,500-11,000), K+ 3.1 mEq/L (ref: 3.5-5.0). Morning cortisol is 65 µg/dL (ref: 6-23) and fails to suppress with high-dose dexamethasone. ACTH is 145 pg/mL (ref: 10-60).

**Which of the following best describes the mechanism of this patient's Cushing syndrome?**

A) Adrenal adenoma secreting cortisol independent of ACTH
B) Autonomous ACTH secretion by a pulmonary neuroendocrine tumor
C) Glucocorticoid receptor upregulation in the hypothalamus
D) Hypothalamic corticotropin-releasing hormone overproduction
E) Pituitary corticotroph adenoma secreting excess ACTH

**Correct Answer: B. Autonomous ACTH secretion by a pulmonary neuroendocrine tumor**

**Explanation:**

This patient has Cushing syndrome secondary to ectopic ACTH production from a pulmonary neuroendocrine tumor (small cell lung carcinoma), which is the most likely cause given the clinical scenario. The key to this diagnosis lies in the laboratory findings: markedly elevated ACTH (145 pg/mL) with high cortisol that fails to suppress with HIGH-dose dexamethasone.

The reasoning chain is: (1) The patient clearly has Cushing syndrome based on the clinical features (moon facies, buffalo hump, central obesity, striae, hypertension, hyperglycemia, hypokalemia, easy bruising). (2) The elevated ACTH indicates this is an ACTH-dependent cause (ruling out adrenal adenoma, which would suppress ACTH). (3) ACTH-dependent Cushing has two main causes: pituitary adenoma (Cushing disease, ~70% of ACTH-dependent cases) and ectopic ACTH production (~20%). (4) Failure to suppress with HIGH-dose dexamethasone points toward ectopic ACTH production, because pituitary corticotroph adenomas typically retain partial suppressibility to high-dose dexamethasone, whereas ectopic tumors are autonomous and do not suppress. (5) The lung mass, heavy smoking history, and rapid onset with hypokalemia further support ectopic ACTH from small cell lung cancer.

**Why each option is incorrect:**
- A) Adrenal adenoma secreting cortisol independent of ACTH — An adrenal adenoma would produce cortisol autonomously, which would SUPPRESS ACTH levels (negative feedback). This patient's ACTH is markedly elevated (145 pg/mL), ruling out any adrenal cause of Cushing syndrome. The misconception tested is failing to use the ACTH level to differentiate ACTH-dependent from ACTH-independent causes.
- C) Glucocorticoid receptor upregulation in the hypothalamus — This is not a recognized pathophysiologic mechanism of Cushing syndrome. Cushing syndrome is caused by excessive cortisol production, not by receptor abnormalities. The hypothalamus regulates cortisol through CRH, and receptor upregulation would not cause the massive cortisol elevation seen here. The misconception tested is fabricating a plausible-sounding but nonexistent mechanism.
- D) Hypothalamic corticotropin-releasing hormone overproduction — While hypothalamic CRH drives pituitary ACTH release, CRH overproduction is a very rare cause of Cushing syndrome and would typically show some suppressibility with dexamethasone (since the pituitary would still retain feedback responsiveness). More importantly, the lung mass and failure to suppress with high-dose dexamethasone point to ectopic ACTH, not hypothalamic dysregulation. The misconception tested is confusing the level of the HPA axis that is dysfunctional.
- E) Pituitary corticotroph adenoma secreting excess ACTH — This is Cushing disease, the most common cause of ACTH-dependent Cushing syndrome. While it would explain elevated ACTH and cortisol, pituitary adenomas typically retain partial feedback responsiveness and therefore show SOME cortisol suppression with high-dose dexamethasone (usually > 50% suppression). The complete failure to suppress with high-dose dexamethasone, combined with the lung mass, heavy smoking history, and markedly elevated ACTH with hypokalemia, all point to ectopic ACTH production rather than a pituitary source. The misconception tested is defaulting to the most common cause (Cushing disease) without interpreting the dexamethasone suppression test results.

**Educational Objective:** Distinguish ectopic ACTH production from pituitary-dependent Cushing disease using clinical and laboratory findings.

**Key Concept:** In ACTH-dependent Cushing syndrome, failure of cortisol to suppress with high-dose dexamethasone strongly suggests ectopic ACTH production (most commonly small cell lung carcinoma), whereas pituitary adenomas (Cushing disease) typically suppress with high-dose dexamethasone. The presence of a lung mass, rapid onset, severe hypokalemia, and very high ACTH levels further favor ectopic production.

## Template 4: Most Likely Complication / Finding

**Lead-in format**: "Which of the following findings is most likely to be present / reported in this patient?" or "Which of the following is the most likely complication?"

**Vignette construction strategy**:
- Present a patient with a known or identifiable condition
- Ask about a complication, associated finding, or expected laboratory/imaging result
- The learner must identify the condition AND know its complications/associations

**Distractor strategy**:
- Distractor 1: A complication of a different but related condition
- Distractor 2: A real association of the condition but not the MOST likely one
- Distractor 3: A finding that would be present in a more advanced or different stage
- Distractor 4: A finding from a different organ system that shares a risk factor

### Example

**Question 4**

A 48-year-old woman presents to the clinic with a 6-month history of generalized itching, fatigue, and darkening of her skin. She also notes that her stools have become pale and bulky, and she has been experiencing right upper quadrant discomfort. She has a history of primary biliary cholangitis diagnosed 3 years ago, for which she takes ursodeoxycholic acid. On examination, there is diffuse hyperpigmentation, xanthelasma around both eyes, and hepatomegaly. The liver is firm and spans 16 cm in the midclavicular line. Laboratory studies show: total bilirubin 3.8 mg/dL (ref: 0.1-1.2), direct bilirubin 2.9 mg/dL (ref: 0-0.3), alkaline phosphatase 420 U/L (ref: 44-147), GGT 385 U/L (ref: 5-40), ALT 68 U/L (ref: 7-56), albumin 2.8 g/dL (ref: 3.5-5.5), INR 1.4 (ref: 0.8-1.2).

**Which of the following laboratory findings is most likely to be present in this patient?**

A) Decreased serum ferritin
B) Elevated serum ceruloplasmin
C) Positive anti-smooth muscle antibody
D) Reduced serum vitamin D, 25-hydroxy level
E) Positive hepatitis B surface antigen

**Correct Answer: D. Reduced serum vitamin D, 25-hydroxy level**

**Explanation:**

This patient has advanced primary biliary cholangitis (PBC) with evidence of cholestasis and early signs of hepatic decompensation (hypoalbuminemia, elevated INR). The key to answering this question lies in understanding the consequences of chronic cholestasis.

Cholestasis — impaired bile flow — leads to malabsorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K) because these vitamins require bile acids in the intestinal lumen for micelle formation and absorption. This patient has clinical evidence of fat malabsorption (pale, bulky stools = steatorrhea), which directly results from inadequate bile acid delivery to the intestine. The vitamin D deficiency is particularly important because it leads to osteomalacia (or osteoporosis), which is one of the most clinically significant complications of chronic cholestatic liver disease. The elevated INR in this patient may also partly reflect vitamin K malabsorption, another fat-soluble vitamin deficiency.

**Why each option is incorrect:**
- A) Decreased serum ferritin — Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant and would be elevated (not decreased) in chronic inflammatory and liver diseases. Additionally, PBC is not primarily associated with iron metabolism abnormalities. Decreased ferritin would suggest iron deficiency, which is not a hallmark of cholestatic liver disease. The misconception tested is confusing the nutritional deficiencies associated with cholestasis (fat-soluble vitamins) with those of other GI conditions.
- B) Elevated serum ceruloplasmin — Ceruloplasmin is DECREASED in Wilson disease, not elevated. In PBC, ceruloplasmin levels are typically normal or mildly elevated (as an acute-phase reactant). Elevated ceruloplasmin is not a characteristic finding of PBC. The misconception tested is confusing Wilson disease (a hepatic copper metabolism disorder) with PBC (an autoimmune cholestatic disease).
- C) Positive anti-smooth muscle antibody — Anti-smooth muscle antibody (ASMA) is associated with autoimmune hepatitis (type 1), not primary biliary cholangitis. PBC is characteristically associated with anti-mitochondrial antibodies (AMA), which are positive in > 95% of PBC patients. The misconception tested is confusing the serologic markers of autoimmune liver diseases — ASMA for autoimmune hepatitis vs. AMA for PBC.
- E) Positive hepatitis B surface antigen — Hepatitis B is a viral hepatitis transmitted through blood or sexual contact, not associated with PBC. While patients with chronic liver disease may have comorbidities, there is no epidemiologic or mechanistic link between PBC and hepatitis B. The misconception tested is attributing liver disease to the most common viral cause without considering the specific autoimmune etiology.

**Educational Objective:** Recognize the metabolic consequences of chronic cholestasis, particularly fat-soluble vitamin deficiencies.

**Key Concept:** Chronic cholestatic liver diseases (such as primary biliary cholangitis and primary sclerosing cholangitis) impair bile acid delivery to the intestine, leading to malabsorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). Vitamin D deficiency is particularly common and clinically significant, as it leads to metabolic bone disease (osteomalacia/osteoporosis) in these patients.

## Template 5: Most Appropriate Next Diagnostic Step

**Lead-in format**: "Which of the following is the most appropriate next diagnostic step?" or "Which of the following tests should be obtained next?"

**Vignette construction strategy**:
- Present a patient where the diagnosis is suspected but not confirmed
- Create a situation where multiple tests could be considered, but one is clearly the most appropriate NEXT step
- The question tests knowledge of diagnostic algorithms and the hierarchy of testing

**Distractor strategy**:
- Distractor 1: A test that is appropriate but comes later in the workup
- Distractor 2: A test that is more invasive when a less invasive option is available and appropriate
- Distractor 3: A test for a different condition in the differential
- Distractor 4: An empiric treatment instead of a diagnostic test (or vice versa)

### Example

**Question 5**

A 58-year-old man presents to his primary care physician with a 2-month history of progressive weakness, weight loss of 7 kg, and intermittent epigastric pain that radiates to his back. He describes the pain as deep and gnawing, partially relieved by leaning forward. He has also noticed that his stools have become greasy and difficult to flush. He has a 30-pack-year smoking history and consumes 3-4 alcoholic drinks daily. On examination, he appears cachectic with temporal wasting. The abdomen is soft with mild epigastric tenderness. There is a palpable, non-tender mass in the right upper quadrant. Laboratory studies show: total bilirubin 4.2 mg/dL (ref: 0.1-1.2), direct bilirubin 3.6 mg/dL (ref: 0-0.3), alkaline phosphatase 380 U/L (ref: 44-147), lipase 85 U/L (ref: 10-140), CA 19-9 480 U/mL (ref: < 37).

**Which of the following is the most appropriate next diagnostic step?**

A) Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP)
B) Fine-needle aspiration biopsy of the pancreatic mass
C) Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC)
D) Positron emission tomography (PET) scan
E) Triphasic contrast-enhanced CT scan of the abdomen

**Correct Answer: E. Triphasic contrast-enhanced CT scan of the abdomen**

**Explanation:**

This patient presents with a clinical picture highly suspicious for pancreatic adenocarcinoma: progressive weight loss, epigastric pain radiating to the back (relieved by leaning forward — a classic feature), steatorrhea (pancreatic exocrine insufficiency), painless jaundice with a palpable gallbladder (Courvoisier sign, suggesting obstruction below the cystic duct), markedly elevated CA 19-9, and significant risk factors (heavy smoking and alcohol use).

The most appropriate next diagnostic step is a triphasic contrast-enhanced CT scan of the abdomen, which is the standard initial imaging study for suspected pancreatic cancer. This study serves multiple purposes: (1) it visualizes the pancreatic mass and assesses its size and location, (2) it evaluates for local invasion of adjacent structures (superior mesenteric artery/vein, celiac axis, portal vein), (3) it identifies metastatic disease (liver metastases, peritoneal deposits), and (4) it determines resectability — the single most important factor in determining treatment approach. The "triphasic" technique (non-contrast, arterial, and venous phases) is specifically designed to characterize pancreatic lesions, which are typically hypovascular and best seen in the venous phase.

**Why each option is incorrect:**
- A) ERCP — While ERCP can visualize the biliary and pancreatic ducts and provides therapeutic options (stent placement for biliary obstruction), it is invasive and carries risks (pancreatitis, hemorrhage, perforation). ERCP is typically performed AFTER CT has characterized the lesion and determined the need for biliary decompression. As an initial diagnostic step, CT is preferred because it provides more comprehensive information about the mass and its relationship to surrounding structures. The misconception tested is choosing a therapeutic/interventional procedure before completing the diagnostic imaging workup.
- B) Fine-needle aspiration biopsy of the pancreatic mass — Tissue diagnosis is required for pancreatic cancer, but the TIMING depends on the clinical situation. If the CT scan shows a clearly resectable tumor in a surgical candidate, many surgeons proceed directly to resection without preoperative biopsy (because a negative biopsy does not exclude cancer and the biopsy itself carries risks). If the tumor is unresectable or if neoadjuvant therapy is planned, then biopsy is needed. Either way, biopsy comes AFTER CT staging. The misconception tested is prioritizing tissue diagnosis over anatomic staging.
- C) Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC) — PTC is used for biliary drainage when ERCP fails or is not feasible. It is not an appropriate initial diagnostic step for suspected pancreatic cancer. PTC visualizes the biliary tree but does not adequately assess the pancreatic mass, local invasion, or metastatic disease. The misconception tested is confusing biliary decompression with cancer staging.
- D) PET scan — While PET/CT can detect metastatic disease and is sometimes used in pancreatic cancer staging, it is not the initial diagnostic test. A contrast-enhanced CT provides better anatomic detail for assessing local invasion and resectability, which is the primary concern at this stage. PET may be used as an adjunct after CT if there is concern for distant metastases not clearly seen on CT. The misconception tested is choosing a functional/metabolic study over the anatomic study that is essential for surgical planning.

**Educational Objective:** Determine the appropriate initial diagnostic study for suspected pancreatic adenocarcinoma.

**Key Concept:** Triphasic contrast-enhanced CT of the abdomen is the first-line imaging study for suspected pancreatic cancer because it simultaneously evaluates the primary tumor, assesses local vascular invasion (which determines resectability), and identifies metastatic disease. ERCP and biopsy are performed after CT staging based on the findings and planned management approach.

## Template 6: Drug of Choice / Most Appropriate Pharmacotherapy

**Lead-in format**: "Which of the following is the most appropriate pharmacotherapy?" or "The drug most likely to be effective in this patient acts by which mechanism?"

**Vignette construction strategy**:
- Present a patient with a condition that has a clearly defined first-line treatment per guidelines
- Include features that specifically favor the correct drug over alternatives
- May include contraindications to other commonly used agents
- The question may also test mechanism of action after identifying the drug

**Distractor strategy**:
- Distractor 1: A second-line or alternative treatment that is appropriate but not first-line
- Distractor 2: A drug used for a similar condition but not the one described
- Distractor 3: A drug that would be contraindicated or harmful in this patient
- Distractor 4: A drug from the same class but not the optimal choice for the specific indication

## Template 7: Epidemiology / Risk Factor Identification

**Lead-in format**: "Which of the following is the most significant risk factor for [condition]?" or "Which additional history finding is most likely to be present?"

**Vignette construction strategy**:
- Present a patient with a condition and ask about the associated risk factor
- Alternatively, present risk factors and ask about the most likely condition
- The learner must understand disease associations and their relative importance

**Distractor strategy**:
- Distractor 1: A risk factor that is associated but less strongly or less specifically
- Distractor 2: A risk factor for a different condition with a similar presentation
- Distractor 3: A commonly believed risk factor that lacks strong evidence
- Distractor 4: A risk factor that is a consequence of the disease rather than a cause

## Template 8: Pathology / Histology Finding

**Lead-in format**: "Which of the following findings is most likely to be seen on biopsy / histologic examination?"

**Vignette construction strategy**:
- Present a patient with a condition that has a characteristic histologic appearance
- The learner must identify the condition AND know its pathologic features
- Include enough clinical information to identify the diagnosis

**Distractor strategy**:
- Distractor 1: A histologic finding from a condition with similar clinical features
- Distractor 2: A finding that is seen in a later or different stage of the same condition
- Distractor 3: A pathologic finding that shares some morphologic features but is from a different disease
- Distractor 4: A finding that is a non-specific marker of inflammation or damage rather than disease-specific

## Combining Templates for Question Sets

When generating a set of questions on a single topic, use different templates to test different cognitive skills:

| Question # | Template | What It Tests |
|------------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | Most likely diagnosis | Pattern recognition from clinical presentation |
| 2 | Mechanism of disease | Pathophysiologic understanding of the diagnosis |
| 3 | Most appropriate next diagnostic step | Diagnostic algorithm knowledge |
| 4 | Drug of choice | Evidence-based treatment knowledge |
| 5 | Most likely complication | Long-term disease understanding |

This ensures the learner is tested on the full clinical spectrum of the topic, from recognition through management to outcomes.
